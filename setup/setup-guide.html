Getting Started

Prerequisites

Make sure you have these accounts and tools ready before starting setup.

Required Accounts & Services

Fluent Community Fluent Free tier available (Pro for messaging, courses)

WordPress community plugin — powers the feed, spaces, profiles, and all backend content. Must be installed on your WordPress site.

Walkthrough: Install Fluent Community on your WordPress site
  1. Go to your WordPress admin → Plugins → Add New.
  2. Search for "FluentCommunity" and click Install Now, then Activate.
  3. If you need Pro features (messaging, courses), purchase a license at fluentcommunity.co and activate it under FluentCommunity → Settings → License.
  4. Once activated, visit FluentCommunity → Settings to configure your community name, spaces, and initial settings.
WordPress required. This app connects to a WordPress site as its backend. You need a WordPress installation (self-hosted or managed) with Fluent Community installed before the app can work.
Expo Account Expo Free tier (30 builds/month)

Cloud build service (EAS) for compiling iOS and Android binaries. Also handles push notification tokens.

Walkthrough: Create an Expo account
  1. Go to expo.dev/signup and create a free account (email + password).
  2. Choose the Free plan — 30 builds/month is more than enough for setup and ongoing updates.
  3. Remember your credentials — you'll use them to log in to EAS later.

Already have an Expo account? You're good — skip ahead.

Free tier is plenty. The free plan gives you 30 builds per month — more than enough for setup and ongoing updates. You only need a paid plan if you're building very frequently.
Apple Developer Account Apple $99/year

Required to submit to the iOS App Store and sign builds.

Walkthrough: Enroll in the Apple Developer Program
  1. Go to developer.apple.com/programs/enroll and sign in with your Apple ID (or create one).
  2. Choose your enrollment type:
    • Individual / Sole Proprietor — apps are listed under your personal name. Simpler signup.
    • Organization — apps are listed under your company name and you can add team members. Requires a D-U-N-S Number (free from Dun & Bradstreet, but can take 5+ business days to issue).
  3. Follow the enrollment steps. You'll need to agree to the Apple Developer Agreement and pay the $99/year fee.
  4. Enrollment can take up to 48 hours to process, so do this early.
Nonprofits & education: Accredited educational institutions and eligible nonprofits may qualify for a fee waiver. Check Apple's fee waiver page before paying.
Walkthrough: Create your app in App Store Connect
  1. Go to appstoreconnect.apple.com/apps and sign in with your Apple Developer account.
  2. Click the blue + button next to "Apps", then select New App (not App Clip or App Bundle).
  3. Fill in the New App form:
    • Platforms — check iOS
    • Name — your app's display name on the App Store (you can change this later)
    • Primary Language — your app's main language
    • Bundle ID — select the bundle ID you registered in the Apple Developer Portal (from the App Identity card). If you haven't registered one yet, do that first and come back here.
    • SKU — an internal reference ID (not visible to users). Use something like your app slug, e.g. mycommunityapp
    • User Access — select Full Access unless you have specific team restrictions
  4. Click Create. Your app now exists in App Store Connect. You'll need the App Store Connect ID later (found under General → App Information → Apple ID).
Don't worry about filling in everything now. You only need the app to exist at this point. Store listing details (screenshots, description, pricing) can wait until you're ready to submit for review.
Google Play Console Google $25 one-time

Required to submit to the Google Play Store.

Walkthrough: Register a Google Play Developer account
  1. Go to play.google.com/console/signup and sign in with your Google account.
  2. Choose your account type:
    • Personal — uses your personal information. Ideal if you don't have a registered business.
    • Organization — requires official business details. Lets you add team members to manage apps.
  3. Accept the Developer Distribution Agreement and pay the $25 one-time registration fee.
  4. Complete your developer profile (name, address, contact info). Google may take a few days to verify your identity.
New account testing requirement: Google now requires new developer accounts to run a closed test with at least 20 testers for 14 consecutive days before you can submit to production. Register your account and start testing early to avoid delays at launch time.
Walkthrough: Create your app in Google Play Console
  1. Go to play.google.com/console and sign in with your Google Play Developer account.
  2. Click Create app on the All Apps page.
  3. Fill in the basics:
    • App name — your app's display name on the Play Store
    • Default language — your app's main language
    • App or Game — select App
    • Free or Paid — select Free (this cannot be changed later)
  4. Check the Developer Program Policies and US export laws declarations, then click Create app.
  5. Your app now exists in the Play Console. The package name gets locked when you upload your first build — make sure it matches the Android Package you set in the App Identity card.
Package name tip: Google Play doesn't ask for a package name during app creation — it's determined by your first uploaded AAB file. The package name comes from the Android Package field in the dashboard. Just make sure you don't change it after your first upload.

Set Up Your Project

Download & Extract

After purchasing, you'll receive a .tar.gz archive (e.g. core-update-3.1.0.tar.gz). Extract it to a folder on your computer and name it after your community — something like MyCommunityApp or FitnessTribeApp. This is your project root.

Open in VS Code

Open VS Code, go to File → Open Folder, and select your project folder. Open the built-in terminal (Terminal → New Terminal or Ctrl+`).

Claude Code — your AI co-pilot

If you installed the Claude Code extension, open it from the sidebar. It reads your project's CLAUDE.md and understands the full codebase — ask it to help with setup, build modules, or debug issues.

Launch the Dashboard

If you're using Claude Code, just ask it to "launch the dashboard". Otherwise, open a terminal in your project folder and run:

npm run dashboard

The dashboard opens http://localhost:3456 in your browser — your setup command center. It reads your actual config files, lets you edit everything in a browser UI, and validates your setup live. Work through each tab from left to right.

Keep the dashboard running while you work through setup. When you're done, click the stop button in the header, or press Ctrl+C in the terminal.
Install Dependencies

If node_modules is missing, the dashboard shows a yellow banner with an Install Dependencies button. Click it — this runs npm install for you. Once complete, the banner disappears and you're ready to configure.

Troubleshooting

npm run dashboard won't start

Port 3456 is likely still in use from a previous session. Run npm run kill-dashboard to free the port, then try again.

npm install fails

Make sure you're running Node.js 18+ (check with node -v). If the error persists, try deleting node_modules and package-lock.json, then run npm install again.

Dashboard opens but shows errors

Make sure you extracted the project files completely and didn't accidentally nest folders (e.g. MyApp/MyApp/). The dashboard expects package.json in the root of the folder you opened.

Config Tab

The Config tab is where you make the app yours. Work through each card top to bottom.

Save as you go. The floating Save Changes bar appears whenever you have unsaved changes. Click it any time — the dashboard writes your config to the right files (app.json, eas.json, app.config.ts, constants/config.ts, package.json) in one go. You don’t need to wait until the end — save after each card if you like.
App Identity

Requires: Apple Google

The App Identity card is where you name your app and set the identifiers the app stores use to find it. You only need to fill in two fields — everything else auto-derives from your app name.

App Name

Type your app name — this is what users see on their home screen and in stores (keep it under 30 characters). The dashboard auto-derives Slug, URL Scheme, APP_NAME, APP_USER_AGENT, and Package Name from it.

iOS Bundle ID

A reverse-domain identifier (e.g. com.yourcompany.community) that uniquely identifies your app in the App Store. Cannot be changed after your first submission — choose carefully. You need to register this in the Apple Developer Portal before building:

Register your Bundle ID in Apple Developer Portal
  1. Go to Certificates, Identifiers & Profiles → Identifiers
  2. Click the blue + button
  3. Select App IDs → Continue → Select App → Continue
  4. Enter a Description and your Bundle ID (Explicit, not Wildcard — must match the dashboard exactly)
  5. Under Capabilities, enable Push Notifications
  6. Click Continue → Register
The bundle ID in the dashboard must match exactly what you registered — mismatches cause build failures and broken push notifications.

Push Notifications

Requires: Firebase

Push notifications require a Firebase project with two config files — one for Android and one for iOS. The dashboard shows a red indicator for each missing file and a green checkmark once uploaded.

1. Create a Firebase project
  1. Go to console.firebase.google.com and sign in with your Google account.
  2. Click Create a project (or Add project).
  3. Enter a project name — use your app name (e.g. “My Community App”). Firebase will generate a project ID below the name field automatically.
  4. On the Google Analytics step, disable the toggle. The app only uses Firebase for push notifications — Analytics is not needed and adds unnecessary complexity.
  5. Click Create project and wait for it to finish provisioning.
2. Add the Android app
  1. From your Firebase project dashboard, click Add app and select the Android icon.
  2. For Android package name, enter the same package name you set in the App Identity card (e.g. com.company.appname). This must match exactly.
  3. For App nickname, enter your app name followed by “Android” (e.g. “My Community App Android”). This helps you tell the two apps apart in Firebase later.
  4. Click Register app.
  5. On the next step (Download and then add config file), click Download google-services.json. Save this file somewhere you can find it.
  6. Click Next to skip through the remaining steps (Add Firebase SDK and Next steps), then click Continue to console. The app already has the Firebase SDK built in — you do not need to edit any Gradle files or add any code.
3. Add the iOS app
  1. Back on the project dashboard, click Add app again and select the iOS icon.
  2. For Apple bundle ID, enter your iOS bundle identifier from the App Identity card (e.g. com.company.appname). This must match exactly.
  3. For App nickname, enter your app name followed by “iOS” (e.g. “My Community App iOS”).
  4. For App Store ID, enter your numeric Apple ID if you already have one — it’s the same App Store Connect ID from the previous step. Find it in App Store Connect → your app → General → App Information → Apple ID. If you haven’t created your app in App Store Connect yet, leave this blank — you can add it later.
  5. Click Register app.
  6. On the next step, click Download GoogleService-Info.plist. Save this file.
  7. Click Next to skip through the remaining steps, then click Continue to console. Same as Android — the SDK is already built into the app.
4. Upload to the dashboard
  1. Back in the setup dashboard, find the Push Notifications card.
  2. Drag and drop google-services.json into the Android upload area. The indicator will turn green with a checkmark.
  3. Drag and drop GoogleService-Info.plist into the iOS upload area. The indicator will turn green with a checkmark.
5. Enable Cloud Messaging
  1. In the Firebase console, click the gear icon next to “Project Overview” and select Project settings.
  2. Click the Cloud Messaging tab.
  3. Look for Firebase Cloud Messaging API (V1) — it should show Enabled with a green checkmark. If it’s already enabled, you’re done.
  4. If it shows Disabled, click the three-dot menu (⋮) on the right side of that row, then click Enable. This may take you to the Google Cloud Console — click Enable there and return to Firebase.
What about the rest of this page? You’ll see sections for APNs Authentication Keys, APNs Certificates, and Web Push certificates — leave these all blank. The app sends push notifications through Expo’s push service, which handles the APNs (Apple) delivery using its own credentials. Firebase is only used for Android delivery via FCM. You also don’t need the Cloud Messaging API (Legacy) — that’s the old deprecated API.
Both config files are required. The app needs google-services.json (Android) and GoogleService-Info.plist (iOS) for push notifications to work on both platforms.
Config tab complete! Make sure you’ve saved your changes before moving on to the next tab.
Troubleshooting

EAS login keeps failing

Try running eas logout first, then eas login again. VPNs can sometimes interfere with Expo's auth — try disconnecting temporarily. Make sure you're using your Expo account credentials (not Apple or Google).

Can't find my App Store Connect ID

In App Store Connect, go to your app → General → App Information. The Apple ID field (a numeric string like 1234567890) is your App Store Connect ID.

Push notifications not working after setup

Both Firebase config files are required — GoogleService-Info.plist (iOS) and google-services.json (Android). Also verify that Firebase Cloud Messaging API (V1) is enabled in your Google Cloud Console. Check the Companion Plugin → Push Log tab for error details.

Save button doesn't appear or changes don't stick

The floating save bar only appears when you make changes. If changes aren't persisting, check your browser console for errors. Try a hard refresh (Ctrl+Shift+R) or a different browser.

Branding Assets assets/images/
iOS App Icon

Your app icon on the iOS home screen, App Store listing, and system UI (notifications, settings, etc.).

  • File: app_icon_ios.png
  • Size: 1024×1024px PNG
  • Transparency: Not allowed — the App Store rejects icons with an alpha channel
  • Corners: Keep them square — iOS applies its rounded mask automatically
  • Content: Fill the entire canvas with your icon; no padding needed
Android Adaptive Foreground

The top layer of your Android app icon. It sits on top of the background layer, and different devices crop the combined result into different shapes (circle, squircle, rounded square).

  • File: app_icon_android_adaptive_fg.png
  • Size: 1024×1024px PNG
  • Transparency: Required — transparent background so the background layer shows through
  • Safe zone: Keep your logo centered in the inner 66% of the canvas — the outer 34% may be cropped

Use icon.kitchen or Android Studio’s Image Asset Studio to preview how your icon looks in different mask shapes.

Android Adaptive Background

The layer behind your foreground icon. You have two options:

  • File: app_icon_android_adaptive_bg.png
  • Solid color (recommended) — use the “Solid Color” toggle on the asset card in the dashboard. This sets adaptiveIcon.backgroundColor in app.json. No image file needed.
  • Image — upload a 1024×1024px PNG. Use this for gradients, patterns, or anything a solid color can’t do.

This layer can shift slightly with parallax effects on some launchers, so keep the design simple and avoid fine details near the edges.

Android Notification Icon

The small icon shown in the Android status bar and notification shade when your app sends a push notification.

  • File: app_icon_android_notification.png
  • Size: 96×96px PNG
  • Transparency: Required — Android uses only the alpha channel (opaque vs transparent pixels). Opaque areas render as white; transparent areas stay clear.
  • Colors: The source image can contain colors, but Android ignores them — only the shape matters
  • Design: Keep it simple and recognizable at small sizes — a silhouette of your logo works best

The Notification Accent Color (set in Branding Colors below) tints the background behind this icon on supported Android versions.

Splash Screen Image

Shown for 1–2 seconds while the app loads. Centered on screen over the splash background color.

  • File: splash_screen_img.png
  • Size: 600×600px PNG recommended
  • Transparency: Recommended — use a transparent background so it looks correct on both the light and dark splash colors
  • Content: Logo mark without text works best at this size and duration

The display width is controlled by imageWidth in the expo-splash-screen plugin config in app.json (default: 200). The background color behind this image is set in Branding Colors below (separate light and dark values).

Login Logo

Your logo on the login and register screens. Use the toggle on the asset card to choose between two modes:

  • Dynamic (default) — The logo is fetched automatically from your Fluent Community site on every app launch (no authentication required). No file upload needed — your site logo is always up to date.
  • Static — Bundle a custom login_logo.png in the app. The server logo still takes priority when available, but this image is used as a fallback (e.g. first launch with no network).

Most users should leave this on Dynamic. Switch to Static only if you need a guaranteed offline fallback image.

  • File: login_logo.png (only needed in Static mode)
  • Size: 750×540px PNG recommended
  • Transparency: Recommended — transparent background adapts to light/dark mode
Login Background

Full-screen background image behind the login and register forms.

  • File: login_background_img.png
  • Size: 1284×2778px PNG recommended
  • Transparency: Not needed — this fills the full screen
  • Design: Dark or blurred images work best so the white text and form fields stay readable

Module System

Modules are self-contained features that plug into the app without touching core code. Enable or disable them by editing modules/_registry.ts.

Enabling/Disabling Modules

// modules/_registry.ts

// Active — uncomment to enable:
import { blogModule } from './blog/module';

// Disabled — comment out to disable:
// import { blogModule } from './blog/module';

export const MODULES: ModuleManifest[] = [
  blogModule,   // Remove this line too when disabling
];
Important: If a module has a tab, it also has a route stub file in app/(tabs)/. The route stub can stay — it's harmless when the module is disabled. But if you want a clean build, remove it too.

Building Your Own Modules

The module system is designed for extensibility. To create your own module:

  1. Create a folder in modules/yourmodule/
  2. Define a manifest in module.ts that exports a ModuleManifest object
  3. Register it in modules/_registry.ts (one import + one array entry)
  4. If your module has a tab, add a route stub in app/(tabs)/ (one-line re-export)

See modules/_types.ts for full TypeScript type definitions and the existing modules for examples.

Troubleshooting

Deep links not working

Verify the .well-known verification files are accessible over HTTPS without redirects. Check that your CDN or caching plugin isn't blocking them. After changing deep linking settings, users may need to reinstall the app for OS-level link association to update.

Maintenance mode not blocking users

Check the Bypass Roles setting — the user's role may be in the bypass list. Also check that the feature is enabled and settings are saved. The app reads maintenance status on each launch.

Smart App Banner not appearing

Requires Deep Linking to be configured with at least the App Name, Bundle ID (iOS), URL Scheme, and App Store ID filled in. The banner only shows on community pages served by Fluent Community.

Push notifications not sending

Check the Push Log tab for errors. Verify Firebase is configured correctly (both platform config files uploaded). Make sure the notification type is enabled on the Notifications tab. Users must have the app installed with push permissions granted.

Feature changes not taking effect

Feature flags are fetched on app launch. Users need to fully close and reopen the app to pick up changes. If you just saved settings, wait a moment for any server-side caching to clear.

First-Time EAS Setup

Before your first build, you need EAS CLI installed and linked to your Expo account.

Setup walkthrough
  1. Install EAS CLI globally: npm install -g eas-cli
  2. Log in to your Expo account: eas login
  3. Initialize the project: eas init — generates a project ID. Copy it to app.json → expo.extra.eas.projectId and set expo.owner to your Expo username.
  4. iOS (first build only): EAS prompts for your Apple Developer login and manages provisioning profiles automatically. See the iOS Credentials card below for the full walkthrough.
  5. Android (first production build): EAS creates and securely stores your signing keystore.
iOS Credentials (first iOS build only)

Before EAS can build for iOS, your Apple Developer account needs a distribution certificate and a provisioning profile for your app's bundle ID. EAS handles this through an interactive command — you only need to run it once per app.

How you'll know you need this: If you click an iOS build button in the dashboard and the build fails with “iOS credentials aren't set up yet,” that's the trigger. The dashboard will show an Open Config to set up button that takes you to the iOS Credentials row in the Config tab — copy the command from there and follow the walkthrough below.
Full credentials setup walkthrough
  1. Open a terminal in your project root and run: 
    eas credentials --platform ios
  2. Pick “Build Credentials” from the menu (manages everything needed to build your project — not Push Notifications or App Store Connect).
  3. Pick “All” when asked what to do (sets up the certificate AND the provisioning profile in one pass).
  4. Sign in to Apple when prompted — your Apple ID email, password, and a 2FA code from your phone.
  5. Distribution certificate: if you already have other apps under the same Apple Developer account, EAS will offer to reuse an existing certificate. Pick yes — Apple limits you to 3 distribution certs at a time and reusing is the right move.
  6. Provisioning profile: pick yes to generate a new one. This is unique to your app's bundle ID and won't affect any other apps you have on the same account.
  7. Device registration: development and preview profiles need at least one registered device (because they install directly to phones, not through the App Store). If you have your iPhone handy, pick yes — EAS will give you a URL to open on your phone that auto-registers it. Otherwise pick no, and either:
    • Run eas device:create later when you have a device to register, or
    • Use the production profile instead, which doesn't need any registered devices (production builds go to the App Store, not directly to phones).
  8. Done. EAS prints “All credentials are ready to build your project.” Pick “Go back” then “Exit” to leave the menu.
  9. Back in the dashboard, click the iOS build button again — this time it should succeed and the build will queue on EAS.
Multi-app accounts: If you build multiple apps under the same Apple Developer account, each app needs its own provisioning profile (they're scoped per bundle ID), but they can all share the same distribution certificate. EAS will detect existing certs and offer to reuse them — always pick reuse to avoid burning your 3-cert limit.
No Apple Developer account? Use the iOS Simulator + Appetize

If you want to see your app running on iOS without setting up an Apple Developer account ($99/year), there's a fully credential-free path:

  1. In the dashboard's Builds tab, click the Simulator button on the iOS card (or use eas build --platform ios --profile simulator from the command line).
  2. EAS builds an iOS Simulator binary (a .app file inside a .tar.gz) — this skips code signing entirely, so no Apple credentials, no certificates, no devices, and no Developer account needed.
  3. When the build finishes, download the .tar.gz from EAS and extract it.
  4. Upload the resulting .app folder to appetize.io — a free browser-based iOS Simulator service.
  5. Open the Appetize URL in your browser to run your app in a simulated iPhone or iPad and take screenshots from your Windows or Linux machine.
What this is good for: showing your app to stakeholders, taking marketing screenshots, verifying the build works before committing to the Apple Developer signup. What it can't do: install on a real iPhone or submit to the App Store — both of those still require a real Apple Developer account and the credentials flow above.

Testing Before Production

Before submitting to production, test your app through each platform's testing track. This catches issues early and — for Google Play — is now mandatory for new accounts.

iOS — TestFlight
  1. After running eas submit --platform ios, your build is uploaded to App Store Connect and automatically appears in TestFlight.
  2. In App Store Connect, go to TestFlight → Internal Testing and add yourself (and any team members) as testers.
  3. Testers receive an email invite — install the TestFlight app from the App Store and accept the invitation to download the build.
  4. Test the app thoroughly. When you're satisfied, submit the build for App Store review from the App Store tab.
Internal vs External testing: Internal testers (up to 100 members of your App Store Connect team) get builds immediately with no review. External testers (up to 10,000 via a public link) require a brief Beta App Review first.
Android — Internal / Closed Testing
  1. After running eas submit --platform android, your build is uploaded to Google Play Console.
  2. Go to Testing → Internal testing (or Closed testing) and create a new release with the uploaded build.
  3. Add tester email addresses or a Google Group under Testers.
  4. Share the opt-in link with testers — they need to accept the invitation and then install via Google Play.
  5. Once testing is complete, promote the release to Production.
New accounts must test first. Google requires new developer accounts to have at least 20 testers opted in for 14 consecutive days in closed testing before you can create a production release. Start this early — it's the #1 cause of launch delays.
Publishing to the Stores

After your build uploads, you still need to complete each store's setup checklist before your app can go live. eas submit handles the binary — but the store listing, content declarations, and review info are on you.

Google Play Console checklist

Google Play requires you to complete several sections before you can create a release. You'll find these under Grow → Store presence and Policy → App content in the Play Console sidebar.

App content (required before release)

  • Privacy policy — provide a URL to your privacy policy page. Host it on your website (e.g. yoursite.com/privacy-policy). It must describe what data the app collects and how it's used.
  • App access — since the app requires login, select "All or some functionality is restricted" and provide test credentials so reviewers can sign in.
  • Ads — select No (the app does not contain ads).
  • Content rating — complete the IARC questionnaire. For a community/social app with user-generated content, answer honestly — this typically results in a Teen or equivalent rating.
  • Target audience — select 18 and over (or your actual target). Do not target under-13 unless you comply with COPPA.
  • Data safety — declare the data your app collects. Key items: account info (name, email), user-generated content (posts, messages, photos), device identifiers (push token). The app does not collect location, contacts, or financial data. Data is not shared with third parties for advertising.
  • Government apps — select No.
  • Financial features — select No unless your community includes purchases/donations.

Store listing

  • App name, short description, full description — describe your community app for users browsing the Play Store.
  • App icon — 512×512 PNG (no transparency). Use your app icon.
  • Feature graphic — 1024×500 JPG or PNG. Displayed at the top of your listing.
  • Screenshots — at least 2 phone screenshots required (recommended: 4–8). Take them from your app running on a device or emulator.
  • App category — select Social (or the category that best fits your community).
  • Contact details — a support email is required. A website and phone number are optional.

Testing & release

  • Closed testing (recommended first) — invite a small group by email to test before going public.
  • Open testing — anyone on Google Play can join your test.
  • Production release — submit to production when ready. First review typically takes 1–3 days.
Google requires 20 testers for 14 days. Before you can submit to production, Google Play now requires your app to have been in closed testing with at least 20 opted-in testers for at least 14 consecutive days. Plan for this lead time.
Apple App Store Connect checklist

In App Store Connect, go to your app and fill in the required sections under the App Store tab before submitting for review.

App information

  • Privacy policy URL — required. Same URL as Google Play.
  • App category — select Social Networking (primary) and optionally a secondary category.
  • Age rating — complete the questionnaire. A community app with user-generated content typically gets a 12+ or 17+ rating.
  • App privacy (nutrition labels) — declare data types collected: contact info (email, name), user content (posts, messages, photos), identifiers (push token). Select "Data not linked to user" for push token. No data is used for tracking.

Store listing

  • Screenshots — required for 6.7" and 5.5" iPhone sizes (at minimum). iPad screenshots if you support tablets.
  • Description — your app's full description.
  • Keywords — up to 100 characters of comma-separated keywords for search.
  • Support URL — a link to your support/contact page.

Review information

  • Sign-in required — check this box and provide demo account credentials so Apple reviewers can log in.
  • Contact information — a phone number and email Apple can reach you at during review.
  • Notes — briefly explain what the app is (e.g. "Private community app for [your org]. Requires an account on our website to log in.").
Apple review typically takes 24–48 hours but can vary. Make sure your demo account works and the app doesn't crash on launch — these are the most common rejection reasons.
Both stores require a privacy policy. You need a publicly accessible URL to a privacy policy page before either store will approve your app. Host it on your website — it should describe what data the app collects (account info, user content, push tokens) and how it's used.

Troubleshooting

Build fails immediately

Run npx expo install --check to detect outdated or incompatible packages, then npx expo install --fix to resolve them. Also check app.json for syntax errors (missing commas, trailing commas, etc.).

eas submit fails for iOS

Verify your Apple Account email and App Store Connect ID are set correctly in the Config tab. You also need an App-Specific Password — generate one at appleid.apple.com under Sign-In and Security → App-Specific Passwords.

eas submit fails for Android

Verify your Service Account Key (.json file) is uploaded in the Config tab. The service account must have the correct permissions in Google Play Console (Users and permissions → Invite user with Admin access). Also ensure the app exists in Google Play Console and you've uploaded at least one build to any testing track.

Build succeeds but app crashes on launch

This usually means a native module mismatch. Check that your app.json plugins array hasn't changed unexpectedly. If you recently added or removed a package, make sure it was a full native build (not an OTA update). Review the crash logs in App Store Connect (Crashes) or Google Play Console (Crashes and ANRs).

App rejected by store review

The most common reasons: (1) the reviewer can't log in — make sure you provided working demo credentials in the review notes, (2) the app crashes on launch, (3) missing privacy policy URL. See the Publishing to the Stores card for the full checklist.

  • The license is automatically activated and linked to your site URL
  • Once activated, the tab displays your plan type and expiration date
  • Your license key is stored locally in setup/.license — excluded from builds and version control

  • The Updates tab automatically checks for available updates when opened (requires an active license)
  • Shows your current version vs the latest available version
  • Displays the changelog for the new version so you can see what changed
  • Click Check Now to manually recheck at any time

Applying Updates

  1. Click Backup & Update when an update is available
  2. The dashboard automatically creates a backup of your current state before applying anything
  3. The update package is downloaded from the license server and applied
  4. Only core files are updated — your customizations are never overwritten

Protected files that are never overwritten by an update:

  • constants/config.ts — your app name, site URL
  • app.json — your bundle IDs, app name, icons, EAS config
  • eas.json — your build profiles and site URL
  • app.config.ts — your fallback values and deep link paths
  • package.json — your package name and any added dependencies
  • assets/images/ — your branding assets
  • modules/ — your custom modules
  • google-services.json / GoogleService-Info.plist — your Firebase configs
After updating: Run npm install to pick up any new or updated dependencies. If the dashboard itself was updated, restart it with npm run dashboard.

Manual Upload

If you received an update package directly (e.g., via email or download), you can apply it manually:

  • Click Upload Package in the Updates tab
  • Select a .tar.gz or .zip update file
  • The same backup and protected-file logic applies — your customizations are safe

Backup & Restore

  • An automatic backup is created before every update, stored in setup/.backups/
  • The dashboard lists available backups with dates and sizes
  • Click Restore on any backup to roll back to that state
  • The last 3 backups are kept; older ones are auto-pruned

manifest.json

The manifest.json file in the project root contains metadata used by the update system:

  • Product version number
  • Update server URL
  • List of protected paths (files that are never overwritten)
  • Core companion plugins list
Do not edit manifest.json manually. It is updated automatically when an update is applied. Editing it could break the update process.
